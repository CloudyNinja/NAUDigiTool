Deciphering the Karnaugh Map

    The Karnaugh Map is  tool that uses the pattern recognition skills of humans to minimize boolean equations.  The Karnaugh Map is effective on problems with up to six variables, and can produce both Sum-of-Product or Product-of-Sum equations.

    Each cell in the Karnaugh map corresponds to a line in the Truth Table.

   

 Each cell is one "bit" different from each of it's neighbors. 

   A cell has as many neighbors as there are variables in the Truth Table. 

   The actual drawing of a Karnaugh Map can vary, as long as each cell remains one bit different from each neighbor. 

 

Groupings

    Groupings in a Karnaugh Map are made by selecting a group of neighboring cells that share the same value.  This can be either all 1's, for Sum-of-Products, or all 0's for Product-of-Sums.  All edges of the grouping must contain a number of cells that is a multiple of 2 (i.e. 1, 2. or 4).  Thus, all groupings will contain a total number of cells that is a power of two (i.e. 1, 2, 4, 8, 32, or 64). 

    As groupings are based on neighboring cells, and not the actual representation of the Karnaugh Map, wrap-around is necessary to ensure minimal groupings. 

    The larger the grouping, the more simpler the equation.  If all the groupings are a large as they can possibly be made, the resulting equation will be minimized.

    Groups may also overlap each other, as long as they follow the above rules.

 

Don't Cares

    A Don't Care is an entry in the Truth Table that, for whatever reason, doesn't matter in to the final equation.  The Don't Care is represented by X's in this particular setup.  A Don't Care can be deemed either a 1 or a 0, whichever is more convenient to supply the simplest boolean equation.
